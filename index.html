Why are we here?

Make street safety understandable in plain language.

Street Smart helps city council members, nonprofits, journalists, teachers, and residents see where streets are most dangerous in Escambia County, what to do first, and how to explain those choices in everyday language.

🫀

Street safety is a health issue — and it isn't shared evenly.

Healthy People 2030, the U.S. national health framework, counts the neighborhood and built environment among the five social determinants of health, with traffic injury a recognized outcome. Crash risk rarely falls evenly: residents in lower-income neighborhoods and households without a car rely more on walking, biking, and transit, putting them in closer, more frequent contact with the county's most dangerous roads. Where U.S. Census demographics are available for a corridor, this tool shows them in the “Who lives here” panel so the equity picture sits beside the crash counts. You can also filter the map by low-income areas below.

      STREET SMART — ESCAMBIA —

      Built on FDOT crash data (2018–2022, all severities) and NHTSA FARS fatals (2022–2024). A prioritization tool — cost and crash-reduction figures are planning estimates, not engineering quotes.

      ▸About the data
      • Corridor GeoJSON — 18 corridors (OSM centerlines) with crash counts, risk scores, matched countermeasures, and plain-language summaries.
      • Crash-density grid — 150 m cells with aggregated FDOT crash counts; cells with fewer than 2 crashes suppressed.
      • Built by the open pipeline in the project repo (scripts/); refreshed monthly from FDOT, NHTSA FARS, and Census ACS sources.
      • Signal Four Analytics — the crash-report database maintained by FDOT and the University of Florida that underlies Florida's official crash records, including the FDOT layers this site uses. It is also the source for the Fairfield Drive / Pensacola Boulevard case study featured above the map.
      ▸Acknowledgements & prior art
      • Safer Streets Priority Finder (Toole Design, MIT license) — an independent, more advanced tool for network-wide safety analysis, including Bayesian predictive risk modeling for road segments with no crash history yet. This site is a separate, independently built codebase (different stack, no shared code) aimed at a simpler, public-facing use case; its "where on this corridor" hotspot feature is a small, simplified analog of SSPF's sliding-window method — dividing a corridor into fixed segments to show which stretch concentrates the most crashes, rather than SSPF's full network-wide, overlapping-window discovery process. SSPF is the tool to reach for if you need to analyze an entire road network from raw crash data, including segments that haven't crashed yet.
      • CyclingMAX (World Bank / ITDP / Progress Analytics) — the conservatism pattern behind this site's benefit-cost estimates (a flat discount applied to all benefits to acknowledge model uncertainty) is adapted from CyclingMAX's "Induced Benefit Factor."
      • The Fairfield Drive / Pensacola Boulevard case study above the map draws on a draft (April 2026) baseline memo Kimley-Horn prepared for the Emerald Coast Regional Council (ECRC, formerly the West Florida Regional Planning Council) under its USDOT Safe Streets and Roads for All grant, and on the ECRC regional high-injury-network StoryMap. Figures there cover 2021–2025 on specific study segments — a different window and extent than this site's own 2018–2022 + FARS corridor counts.
      • Road centerlines: OpenStreetMap contributors. Crash data: FDOT, NHTSA FARS, Signal Four Analytics. Demographics: US Census Bureau ACS. Countermeasures: FHWA Proven Safety Countermeasures and CMF Clearinghouse.
